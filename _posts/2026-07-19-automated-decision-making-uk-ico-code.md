Automated systems increasingly influence decisions about employment, credit, public services and access to other important opportunities. These systems may improve efficiency and consistency, but they also raise concerns about transparency, accountability and people’s ability to challenge decisions that significantly affect them.

The Data (Use and Access) Act 2025 changed the UK’s legal framework for significant decisions made solely through automated processing. It expanded the circumstances in which organisations may make these decisions using ordinary personal data, while retaining safeguards intended to protect individuals.

In 2026, the government formally required the Information Commissioner’s Office to prepare a statutory Code of Practice covering artificial intelligence and automated decision-making. The future Code could become an important part of the UK’s approach to balancing technological innovation with data protection and public trust.
This Policy Note explains the changing legal framework and considers three practical issues that the Code will need to address:
1. explaining automated decisions;
2. ensuring that human intervention is meaningful; and
3. supporting responsible innovation through regulatory coordination and sandboxes.

## What is automated decision-making?
Automated decision-making occurs when a decision is made through automated processing without meaningful human involvement.

Although artificial intelligence can be used to make automated decisions, the term is broader than AI. Automated decision-making may also rely on conventional algorithms, scoring systems or rules-based software.
Examples include:
* software screening and ranking job applicants;
* automated credit-scoring systems approving or rejecting applications;
* insurance systems calculating eligibility or premiums;
* public-sector systems assessing entitlement to services or identifying possible fraud; and
* online platforms automatically restricting, prioritising or removing content.

Not every automated process falls within the most restrictive data-protection provisions. The legal framework is particularly concerned with decisions based solely on automated processing that produce legal effects or similarly significant effects for an individual.

A decision might be significant where it substantially affects someone’s employment, finances, education, access to services or other important interests.

## How has the legal framework changed?
Before the Data (Use and Access) Act 2025, Article 22 of the UK General Data Protection Regulation generally restricted solely automated decisions that produced legal or similarly significant effects.
Such decisions were permitted only in limited circumstances, including where they were:
* necessary for entering into or performing a contract;
* authorised by law; or
* based on the individual’s explicit consent.

The 2025 Act replaced this framework with new provisions covering significant decisions based solely on automated processing.
For ordinary personal data, organisations can now rely on the wider range of lawful bases available under UK data-protection law. This gives organisations greater flexibility to use automated systems, but it does not remove their responsibilities.

Where a significant decision is made solely through automated processing, organisations must provide safeguards for the individual’s rights, freedoms and legitimate interests. These include measures that allow the individual to:
* receive information about the automated decision;
* make representations about it;
* obtain human intervention; and
* contest the decision.

Stricter conditions continue to apply where a significant automated decision is based on special-category personal data, such as information about health, ethnicity, biometric identification, political opinions or religious beliefs.
The reforms therefore represent a shift rather than a removal of regulation. The previous framework began with a general restriction and specified limited exceptions. The revised framework gives organisations more room to use automated decision-making involving ordinary personal data, while placing greater emphasis on safeguards, accountability and redress.

## A statutory Code of Practice
The Data Protection Act 2018 (Code of Practice on Artificial Intelligence and Automated Decision-Making) Regulations 2026 came into force on 12 May 2026.
These Regulations require the Information Commissioner to prepare a Code of Practice giving guidance on good practice in:
* developing and using artificial intelligence; and
* conducting automated decision-making involving personal data.

The Code must also address good practice in the processing of children’s personal data.

The Regulations themselves primarily impose a duty on the Information Commissioner to prepare the Code. Organisations’ underlying legal obligations continue to arise from the UK GDPR, the Data Protection Act 2018 and related legislation.

However, the Code could play an influential role in explaining how these requirements should be applied in practice. It may help organisations understand what regulators expect in areas including transparency, explainability, discrimination, accountability, individual rights and routes to redress.

The ICO consulted on updated guidance concerning automated decision-making and profiling in 2026. It has indicated that this guidance will inform parts of the future statutory Code.

## Challenge 1: Explaining automated decisions
Transparency is one of the central challenges associated with automated decision-making.

People affected by significant automated decisions need information that enables them to understand what has happened and exercise their rights. However, producing a useful explanation can be difficult, particularly where an organisation uses a technically complex model.

Some machine-learning systems are described as “black boxes” because the relationship between their inputs, internal processes and outputs may be difficult to interpret. Modern deep-learning models can involve large numbers of interacting parameters, making it difficult to identify a simple reason for an individual output.

This does not mean that every organisation must provide a complete technical account of every mathematical operation. A highly technical explanation might be accurate but still be incomprehensible to the person affected.

An effective explanation should instead be appropriate to its audience and purpose. Depending on the context, it could include:
* the purpose of the system;
* the principal factors that influenced the decision;
* the personal data used;
* the source and quality of that data;
* the role of the automated system;
* the identity of the organisation responsible;
* the possible consequences of the decision; and
* the process for requesting review or challenging the outcome.

The ICO and the Alan Turing Institute’s earlier guidance on explaining decisions made with AI identified several relevant forms of explanation. These included explanations concerning rationale, responsibility, data, fairness, safety and performance, and impact.

The future Code could build on this work by clarifying what level of explanation is expected in different settings. A recruitment decision, for example, may require a different explanation from a fraud-detection alert or an automated recommendation with limited consequences.

The challenge is therefore not simply to make an algorithm technically interpretable. It is to give affected individuals information that is understandable, relevant and useful.

## Challenge 2: Making human intervention meaningful
The right to obtain human intervention is an important safeguard. However, the presence of a person in a review process does not automatically make that safeguard effective.
A reviewer may accept an automated recommendation without properly examining it. This is sometimes associated with automation bias: the tendency to place excessive confidence in an automated output because it appears objective or technically sophisticated.

Human intervention risks becoming a “rubber stamp” where the reviewer:
* lacks the authority to change the decision;
* does not understand how the system works;
* has insufficient time or information;
* is evaluated according to how quickly cases are processed;
* rarely challenges the automated recommendation; or
* assumes that the system is more accurate than human judgement.

Meaningful intervention should involve more than confirming that an automated process has been followed. The reviewer should be able to examine the relevant evidence, consider information supplied by the individual, identify possible errors and alter the outcome where appropriate.
Organisations may therefore need to consider:
* who is qualified to conduct reviews;
* what information reviewers receive;
* whether reviewers understand the system’s limitations;
* whether they have sufficient independence and authority;
* how disagreements with automated outputs are recorded; and
* whether review outcomes are monitored.

The ICO’s Code could provide practical examples of meaningful intervention across different sectors. Such examples would be particularly valuable for smaller organisations that may not have dedicated legal, technical and compliance teams.

## Opportunity: Supporting innovation without weakening safeguards
The UK’s broader approach to AI regulation has emphasised innovation, sectoral expertise and the use of existing regulators.
Under this model, regulators such as the ICO, Financial Conduct Authority, Competition and Markets Authority and sector-specific bodies address AI-related risks within their respective areas of responsibility.

This approach can offer flexibility, but it may also create difficulties where an AI system operates across several sectors. A single product might process financial, health, employment and other personal data, bringing it within the interests of multiple regulators.
Regulatory sandboxes are one method of helping organisations navigate this environment.

A sandbox allows participating organisations to test an innovative product or service with regulatory support and oversight. Sandboxes can help developers identify risks, understand legal requirements and incorporate safeguards before wider deployment.

The ICO already operates a Regulatory Sandbox supporting organisations that use personal data in innovative products and services. The government has also developed proposals for an AI Growth Lab intended to support responsible AI innovation across the economy.
Well-designed sandboxes could help organisations:
* identify data-protection risks earlier;
* test safeguards before full deployment;
* improve communication between developers and regulators;
* understand how different regulatory requirements interact;
* generate evidence about emerging technologies; and
* incorporate data protection by design.

However, regulatory sandboxes should not be treated as exemption zones in which fundamental protections no longer apply. Participation should be accompanied by clear eligibility criteria, appropriate oversight, transparent learning and effective safeguards for people affected by testing.

Access is another consideration. Large organisations may be better placed to participate because they have specialist staff and resources. The government and regulators may therefore need to consider how start-ups and small organisations can obtain meaningful support without facing disproportionate administrative burdens.
